# Design Philosophy

The judgment calls that don't reduce to a checklist. Techniques and references tell you *what's available*; this is about *how to choose* among them.

## Boldness and restraint are not opposites — they're a ratio

A piece that's bold everywhere is exhausting and reads as unconfident (as if it doesn't trust any single element to carry weight, so it makes everything shout). A piece that's restrained everywhere reads as safe and forgettable. The professional move is concentration: spend real boldness in exactly one or two places — a signature layout move, an unexpected but justified color, a typographic pairing with real character — and keep everything else quiet and disciplined around it. The restraint is what makes the boldness legible as a choice rather than noise.

Ask directly: *if someone remembers one thing about this piece, what should it be?* If you can't answer, you likely haven't taken a real risk yet. If you can name three things, you've taken too many and they're competing.

## Handmade vs. templated is about specificity, not effort

A "templated" feel doesn't come from using a grid or a system — systems are good. It comes from choices that could apply to literally any piece of similar content. A handmade feel comes from choices that are specific to *this* brief: this data, this audience, this subject's own vocabulary and materials.

Concretely: before finalizing a direction, ask "would I make this same choice for a different client/topic in this same category?" If the honest answer is yes, the choice is a default, not a decision — even if it's a *tasteful* default. Push until at least the signature element is something that genuinely comes from this specific content.

The fastest way to find specific material: look at the subject itself. A financial report about a manufacturing company can draw on industrial materials/forms; a dashboard for a music platform can draw on audio-visual vocabulary (waveforms, EQ-style bars); a report on agricultural data can draw on the actual rhythms of that domain (seasons, yield curves) rather than a generic corporate blue dashboard. Generic subjects get generic treatment by default — the antidote is grounding, not more decoration.

## Professionalism is a floor, not a ceiling

Every deliverable should clear a baseline regardless of how adventurous the direction is: legible contrast, consistent alignment, correct data, no orphaned tool-default elements sitting next to styled ones, readable at the sizes/contexts it'll actually be viewed in (a slide seen on a projector, a dashboard on a laptop, a report printed or read as PDF). This floor is non-negotiable and doesn't depend on how much creative risk the rest of the piece takes. A wildly creative piece with a broken grid or illegible contrast is still a failure; a very safe piece that nails the floor is at least usable, even if forgettable.

Between "meets the floor" and "excellent," the difference is almost always: (1) a real system was defined and followed, (2) hierarchy was considered deliberately rather than left to reading order, and (3) something specific to this content was found and used as the signature move. None of these require exotic technique — they require actually stopping to decide instead of shipping the first pass.

## Matching complexity to the vision

An elaborate maximalist direction needs elaborate, careful execution — lots of moving parts, all of them controlled — or it collapses into clutter. A minimal direction needs precision, not just absence — every remaining element has to be exactly right, because there's nowhere to hide an off spacing value or an awkward line break. Neither direction is inherently "more professional"; what reads as unprofessional is a mismatch — an ambitious, busy layout executed sloppily, or a minimal layout that's actually just unfinished rather than intentionally spare.

Decide the register early (how much is this piece allowed to say visually?) and hold every subsequent choice to that register, rather than letting complexity drift up (one more chart, one more accent color, one more decorative element) over the course of building it.

## Knowing when good is done

Perfectionism has a cost: a piece can be iterated into fussiness, where every element has been individually optimized but the whole has lost coherence, or where diminishing-return polish delays a deliverable the user needed now. Treat the checklist and elevation protocol as tools to reach "clearly, defensibly good" — not as a mandate to iterate indefinitely. If the user's request signals urgency or low stakes ("just get me something quick"), a lighter single pass with good defaults from `technique-catalog.md` is the right call, and it's fine to say so rather than silently under- or over-delivering relative to what they actually need.
