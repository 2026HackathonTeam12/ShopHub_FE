# Reference Library

Concrete design worlds to draw from, and — more importantly — *what specifically to borrow from each*, so references become decisions instead of mood-board name-dropping. Naming "Stripe-inspired" without saying what that means produces nothing; naming the specific mechanism does.

Pick 1-3 of these per deliverable, matched to the brief's audience and purpose. Mixing more than 2-3 tends to produce an incoherent piece; each has an internal logic that clashes with the others if blended carelessly.

## Product / SaaS references

**Stripe**
- What to borrow: generous whitespace, restrained single-accent color use against neutral/white, precise small type for technical detail, subtle gradients used only in illustrative diagrams (never on buttons/cards), a strong grid with consistent section rhythm.
- Fits: technical dashboards, developer-facing docs, B2B product decks, financial reports.
- Don't borrow: their exact purple/indigo — pick your own accent that fits the brief.

**Linear**
- What to borrow: near-black dark mode done right (not pure black), extremely tight and confident type scale, minimal chrome, purposeful micro-interactions, monochrome iconography, information density handled through hierarchy rather than size alone.
- Fits: internal tools, engineering-facing dashboards, product roadmaps, anything that should feel "fast" and precise.

**Apple (marketing pages / keynote decks)**
- What to borrow: one hero statement per screen/slide, huge confident type paired with enormous negative space, product-as-hero photography/rendering, restraint to the point of starkness, smooth large-scale motion for transitions.
- Fits: product launches, executive-facing decks, anything where the message needs to feel inevitable and singular rather than dense with detail.
- Don't borrow: this only works with genuinely strong hero content — a weak headline in giant type just looks empty. Don't use this direction to hide thin content.

## Editorial / print references

**Swiss / International Typographic Style (Müller-Brockmann)**
- What to borrow: rigorous grid systems, sans-serif (Helvetica/Akzidenz-family or a modern equivalent) set with mathematical precision, asymmetric but grid-locked layouts, red or black as the sole accent, objective/neutral tone with no decoration.
- Fits: data-heavy reports, research summaries, anything that should read as authoritative and objective rather than persuasive or warm.

**Bauhaus**
- What to borrow: geometric shape as structural element (not decoration), primary-color-plus-black-and-white restraint, functional honesty (form follows content, no ornament that doesn't do a job), bold geometric type treatments for headlines.
- Fits: bold conceptual decks, education/explainer content, anything that benefits from a confident, slightly retro-modernist voice.

**Financial Times / The Economist (editorial data journalism)**
- What to borrow: a signature serif or slab for headlines paired with a clean sans for body/data, muted specific accent colors (FT's salmon-adjacent tones, Economist's red), chart annotation directly on the visual, confident use of a single strong chart per section rather than many small ones.
- Fits: research reports, market analyses, anything with real editorial narrative around data.

## Dashboard / data-product references

**Modern BI tools done well (e.g., well-designed Tableau/Looker/Observable dashboards)**
- What to borrow: consistent card system with clear metric hierarchy (big number, small trend, smaller context), muted neutral backgrounds so data colors pop, tonal color ramps for sequential data, restrained use of red/green only for true status.
- Fits: internal dashboards, sales/ops reporting, KPI summaries.

## When no single reference fits

If the brief is genuinely novel (a niche subject, an unusual audience), don't force it into one of the above. Instead, ground the design in the subject matter itself: what does this specific domain's own visual vernacular look like (its instruments, materials, existing artifacts), and take cues from there rather than a generic "professional" default. This is often the strongest source of distinctiveness — see `design-philosophy.md`.

## Anti-patterns to recognize and avoid

These are the current default "AI-generated" tells — recognizing them is as useful as knowing the good references:
- Warm cream background (~#F4F1EA) + high-contrast serif display + terracotta/clay accent (~#D97757).
- Near-black background with a single bright acid-green or vermilion accent, used indiscriminately.
- Broadsheet-style dense columns with hairline rules and zero border-radius, applied regardless of subject.
- Centered titles over a full-bleed gradient hero on every slide of a deck.
- Default Excel/PowerPoint categorical color cycling (blue, orange, gray, yellow, dark blue...) on every chart.
- Numbered markers (01/02/03) applied to content that isn't actually sequential.
- A card grid with a drop shadow and rounded corners on literally every element, with no variation.
